# AutoQuant Trading Definitions

This document defines the official trading evaluation rules for AutoQuant. It establishes how strategies are classified, validated, and evaluated for acceptance.

## Core Philosophy
AutoQuant does not treat one profitable backtest as proof that a strategy is truly profitable. Strategies are judged through measurable evidence, robustness, and risk-aware validation.

## Term Definitions

### 1. Strategy Idea
A conceptual trading approach or hypothesis provided by the user. This can be a description of a trading logic, a set of rules, or an inspiration for a strategy. Ideas are not yet code and require implementation before validation.

### 2. Strategy Candidate
A concrete, implementable strategy that has been coded and can be tested. A candidate has moved from idea to executable code with defined parameters, entry/exit logic, and risk management rules.

### 3. Profitable Backtest
A single historical simulation that shows positive net profit over a specific time period. One profitable backtest is not sufficient evidence of a truly profitable strategy and must be validated through additional tests.

### 4. Profitable Strategy
A strategy that has demonstrated consistent positive performance across multiple validation dimensions including backtests, out-of-sample testing, walk-forward analysis, and robustness checks. A profitable strategy is never guaranteed to remain profitable in the future.

### 5. Positive Expectancy
A mathematical measure of the average profit or loss per trade. Positive expectancy means that, on average, each trade contributes to account growth. Expectancy accounts for both win rate and the magnitude of wins versus losses.

### 6. Profit Factor
The ratio of gross profits to gross losses. A Profit Factor above 1.0 indicates that profits exceed losses. Higher values suggest better risk-adjusted performance, but extremely high values may indicate overfitting.

### 7. Net Profit
The total profit or loss generated by a strategy over the testing period, including all wins, losses, fees, and slippage. Net profit must be positive after all costs for a strategy to be considered viable.

### 8. Max Drawdown
The maximum peak-to-trough decline in account value during the testing period. Drawdown measures the largest loss from a peak and is a critical risk metric. Strategies must keep drawdown within acceptable limits.

### 9. Sharpe / Calmar Style Risk-Adjusted Thinking
Risk-adjusted performance metrics that evaluate returns relative to risk taken. Sharpe ratio measures return per unit of volatility, while Calmar ratio measures annualized return relative to maximum drawdown. AutoQuant prioritizes strategies with favorable risk-adjusted metrics.

### 10. Trade Count
The total number of trades executed during the testing period. Trade count must be sufficient to provide statistical significance. Strategies with too few trades cannot be reliably validated even if individual trades are profitable.

### 11. Win Rate
The percentage of winning trades relative to total trades. Win rate alone is insufficient for strategy evaluation and must be considered alongside average win/loss magnitude and overall expectancy.

### 12. Average Win / Average Loss
The mean profit of winning trades and the mean loss of losing trades. The relationship between average win and average loss, combined with win rate, determines expectancy. A strategy can have a low win rate but still be profitable if average wins significantly exceed average losses.

### 13. Fees and Slippage Awareness
Recognition that real trading costs reduce profitability. Strategies must be evaluated with realistic fee structures and slippage assumptions. Net profit calculations must account for these costs, and strategies that are only profitable without fees are rejected.

### 14. Out-of-Sample Validation
Testing a strategy on data that was not used during strategy development or optimization. OOS validation checks whether a strategy's performance generalizes to unseen time periods and helps detect overfitting.

### 15. Walk-Forward Validation
A rolling window validation approach where a strategy is optimized on a training period and tested on a subsequent validation period. This process repeats across the dataset to assess stability and performance consistency over time.

### 16. Robustness Check
Tests that evaluate whether a strategy remains stable under varying conditions. This includes parameter sensitivity analysis, performance across different market conditions, and testing on multiple timeframes or pairs.

### 17. Overfitting
When a strategy is excessively tuned to historical data and captures noise rather than genuine market patterns. Overfitted strategies perform well on training data but fail on new data. AutoQuant actively detects and rejects overfitted strategies.

### 18. Lookahead Bias
When a strategy inadvertently uses information that would not have been available at the time of trading decision. Lookahead bias creates unrealistically good backtest results and must be eliminated for valid validation.

### 19. Recursive Indicator Issues
Problems that arise when indicators are calculated using data that includes the current bar's values, creating implicit lookahead bias. Recursive calculations must be carefully structured to avoid using future information.

### 20. Controlled Failure
Deliberate testing of strategies under adverse conditions to assess their resilience. Controlled failure tests evaluate how strategies perform during market stress, parameter variations, and unfavorable conditions to understand their breaking points.

## Official Profitability Logic

A strategy is not considered accepted just because one backtest is positive. A strategy can only move toward acceptance if it has:

- **Positive net profit after fees**: The strategy must generate profit after accounting for all trading costs
- **Profit Factor above the configured threshold**: Gross profits must exceed gross losses by the required margin
- **Max drawdown below the configured threshold**: Peak-to-trough decline must remain within acceptable risk limits
- **Enough trades for the timeframe and timerange**: Trade count must provide statistical significance
- **Positive expectancy**: Average profit per trade must be positive
- **Reasonable average win/loss behavior**: The relationship between win size and loss size must be sustainable
- **No lookahead bias**: Strategy must not use future information in decision-making
- **No obvious overfitting**: Performance must generalize across different conditions
- **Acceptable out-of-sample behavior**: Strategy must perform on unseen data
- **Acceptable walk-forward behavior**: Performance must be consistent across rolling validation periods
- **Acceptable robustness behavior**: Strategy must remain stable under parameter variations
- **Reasonable performance across selected pairs**: Strategy must work across the intended trading universe

## Classification Levels

### 1. Rejected
Strategies that fail fundamental validation requirements and cannot proceed to further testing.

**Rejection criteria:**
- Failed core validation (syntax errors, runtime errors, invalid logic)
- Not enough data for meaningful testing
- Not enough trades for statistical significance
- Negative expectancy (average loss per trade)
- Excessive drawdown beyond configured thresholds
- Bad Profit Factor (losses exceed profits or margin too thin)
- Broken strategy code (crashes, hangs, produces invalid signals)
- Lookahead/recursive safety issues (uses future information)
- Overfit or unstable behavior (fails robustness checks)

### 2. Candidate
Strategies that pass basic minimum thresholds but require additional validation before advancing.

**Candidate criteria:**
- Basic backtest passes minimum profitability thresholds
- Positive net profit after fees
- Trade count meets minimum requirements for timeframe
- No obvious code errors or safety issues
- Needs more validation (OOS, WFA, robustness)
- Not ready for live/dry-run recommendation
- Metrics are marginal and need confirmation

### 3. Promising
Strategies that demonstrate better metrics than Candidates and have passed additional validation checks.

**Promising criteria:**
- Better metrics than Candidate level
- Stronger Profit Factor and drawdown performance
- Has passed additional validation checks
- Shows consistent behavior across initial tests
- Still needs walk-forward analysis (WFA) confirmation
- Still needs out-of-sample (OOS) confirmation
- Still needs comprehensive robustness checks
- Not yet ready for export

### 4. Validated
Strategies that have passed all major validation gates and can be exported for further review.

**Validated criteria:**
- Passed core backtest with strong metrics
- Passed out-of-sample validation
- Passed walk-forward analysis
- Passed robustness checks
- Acceptable performance across selected pairs
- Still not guaranteed to be profitable in live trading
- Can be exported for further manual review
- Can be exported for dry-run testing
- Requires user judgment before live deployment

### 5. Approved
The highest internal classification AutoQuant can assign. Represents the strongest evidence available within the system.

**Approved criteria:**
- Highest internal classification available
- Strongest evidence available inside AutoQuant
- Excellent performance across all validation dimensions
- Consistent behavior in OOS, WFA, and robustness tests
- Favorable risk-adjusted metrics (Sharpe/Calmar style)
- Reasonable parameter sensitivity
- Strong performance across pair universe
- Still not a guarantee of future profit
- Still requires user judgment and risk management
- Represents the best available candidate for deployment

## Default Threshold Examples (Configurable)

All thresholds are configurable and should be adjusted based on:
- Trading timeframe (scalping vs swing trading)
- Market conditions (volatility regime)
- User risk tolerance
- Specific pair characteristics

Part 06 centralizes the current baseline thresholds in `DecisionPolicyService` and documents them in `docs/DECISION_POLICIES.md`. The values below are general trading examples; the active Part 06 defaults are the decision-policy thresholds.

**Minimum Trade Count (depends on timeframe):**
- Scalping (1m-5m): 200+ trades minimum
- Day trading (15m-1h): 100+ trades minimum
- Swing trading (4h-daily): 50+ trades minimum
- Position trading (weekly+): 25+ trades minimum

**Profit Factor Thresholds:**
- Candidate: 1.20+ (initial viability)
- Promising: 1.30+ (stronger evidence)
- Validated: 1.40+ (robust performance)
- Approved: 1.50+ (excellent risk-adjusted returns)

**Max Drawdown Thresholds:**
- Candidate: < 30% (basic risk control)
- Promising: < 25% (improved risk management)
- Validated: < 20% (strong risk control)
- Approved: < 15% (excellent risk management)

**Expectancy Requirements:**
- All levels: Must be positive
- Candidate: Small positive acceptable
- Promising: Moderate positive expected
- Validated: Strong positive expectancy
- Approved: Excellent positive expectancy

**Net Profit Requirements:**
- All levels: Must be positive after fees
- Strategies with too few trades cannot be accepted even if profit is high
- Profit must be proportional to risk taken and trade count

**Additional Notes:**
- Strategies with too few trades cannot be accepted even if profit is high
- Extremely high Profit Factors (> 3.0) may indicate overfitting and require scrutiny
- Thresholds should be calibrated based on historical performance of accepted strategies
- Part 06 thresholds are acceptance gates for parsed evidence and are not profit guarantees

## Expectancy Formula

**Expectancy = (Win Rate × Average Win) - (Loss Rate × Absolute Average Loss)**

Where:
- Win Rate = Percentage of winning trades (0 to 1)
- Average Win = Mean profit of winning trades
- Loss Rate = Percentage of losing trades (0 to 1) = 1 - Win Rate
- Absolute Average Loss = Mean loss of losing trades (expressed as positive number)

**Example:**
- Win Rate: 45% (0.45)
- Average Win: $150
- Average Loss: $100
- Expectancy = (0.45 × $150) - (0.55 × $100) = $67.50 - $55 = $12.50 per trade

A positive expectancy indicates that, on average, each trade contributes to account growth.

## Weekly Profit Targets

AutoQuant must not optimize blindly for extreme weekly return targets. A user target like 15% weekly profit is treated as a high-risk objective, not a promise.

**Principles:**
- Weekly profit targets are user-specified goals, not system promises
- High weekly return targets (e.g., 15%+) are inherently high-risk objectives
- The system must prioritize survivability over unrealistic return promises
- Drawdown control takes precedence over return maximization
- Sufficient trade count is more important than maximizing individual trade returns
- Robustness across conditions is valued over peak performance in one scenario

**System Behavior:**
- AutoQuant will flag strategies that meet high weekly targets but show signs of overfitting
- Strategies with extreme returns but poor risk metrics will be rejected
- The system prioritizes consistent, sustainable performance over volatile high returns
- Users are warned that high weekly targets increase the risk of strategy failure
- Validation focuses on whether the strategy can survive adverse conditions, not just achieve peak returns

**Risk-Adjusted Approach:**
- A strategy achieving 5% weekly with 10% drawdown is preferred over 15% weekly with 40% drawdown
- Consistent 8% weekly returns across multiple pairs is preferred over 20% returns on one pair
- Strategies that survive walk-forward analysis are valued over strategies that peak in one period
- The system emphasizes that high returns without high risk is unrealistic in trading

## Summary

AutoQuant evaluates strategies through a multi-dimensional validation framework. No single metric determines acceptance. Strategies must demonstrate consistent performance across backtesting, out-of-sample testing, walk-forward analysis, and robustness checks. The classification system provides clear progression from idea to approved strategy, with each level requiring stronger evidence of viability. All thresholds are configurable to accommodate different trading styles and risk preferences.
